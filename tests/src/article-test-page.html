Video shows US Tomahawk missile hitting near Iran school that killed 175

Newly released video footage, authenticated by the investigative outlet Bellingcat, shows a US Tomahawk cruise missile striking an Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps naval facility in Minab, Iran, on February 28 — the same location where a girls' elementary school was destroyed, killing at least 175 people, most of them children between the ages of 7 and 12.

The footage, originally released by Iran's Mehr News Agency and geolocated by Bellingcat, also captures smoke already rising from the vicinity of the Shajareh Tayyebeh girls' school at the time the missile hits the adjacent IRGC compound. Bellingcat noted that the United States is the only participant in the conflict known to possess Tomahawk missiles, and that Israel does not operate the weapon. The findings add to a growing body of evidence from The New York Times, CNN, CBC, and NPR suggesting the US military was responsible for the deadliest single strike on civilians since the war began on February 28.cnn+3

Trump Blames Iran as Evidence Points to US

The video's release came one day after President Trump, speaking aboard Air Force One on March 7, blamed Iran for the school attack. "We think it was done by Iran. Because they are very inaccurate, as you know, with their munitions. They have no accuracy whatsoever," Trump told reporters, according to Politico. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth added that "The only side that targets civilians is Iran" while noting an investigation remains active.politico+1

Those claims have been challenged by multiple independent investigations. Reuters reported on March 5, citing two US military officials familiar with an internal probe, that American investigators believe US forces likely carried out the strike. CNN's analysis found the school was hit at the same time as precision strikes on the adjacent IRGC base, with geolocated videos showing smoke rising from both sites simultaneously.

International Condemnation

Human Rights Watch called for the strike to be investigated as a war crime. UNESCO condemned the bombing as "grave violation of humanitarian law," warning that "attacks against educational institutions endanger students and teachers and undermine the right to education". UN High Commissioner for Human Rights Volker Türk said the UN has "significant concerns" about whether the strikes complied with international humanitarian law.Human Rights Watch+2
